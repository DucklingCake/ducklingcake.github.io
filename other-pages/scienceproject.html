What is CRISPR-Cas9?

CRISPR-Cas9 (or just CRISPR for short) is a tool that enables geneticists and scientific researchers to edit genomes by changing the DNA sequence. CRISPR stands for Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeat. It is currently the simplest form of gene manipulation.

How does CRISPR work?

CRISPR uses Cas9 and RNA to work. Cas9 is an enzyme that is used as 'molecular scissors.' It can cut genomes so parts can be added or removed. The RNA is a special type of RNA called a guide RNA or a gRNA. It makes sure Cas9 cuts off the right part of the genome. 

The first step of CRISPR is when gRNA finds and binds a specific DNA sequence. Next, the Cas9 genome follows the gRNA to the DNA sequence where it cuts across both strands of the DNA. The cell automatically tries to repair the DNA, and this is when scientists can introduce changes to the damaged genomes.

How was it developed?

Scientists learned that some bacteria, including e coli, have a built in gene editing system. Bacteria has this so they are able to quickly respond to pathogens including some viruses. These bacteria snip out the viruses, but the leave some behind so they are able to defend against is better if it comes in the future. Scientists took some of this technology and developed CRISPR to allow gene editing in animals.
